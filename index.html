About

Who I Am

I am a PhD researcher in Computer Engineering at Stony Brook University, where I study how AI can help us understand and improve human collaboration in engineering education. My work sits at the intersection of speech analytics, natural language processing, behavioral science, and neurosymbolic AI.

Before my PhD, I spent over 10 years in industry building enterprise software, AI-powered automation systems, and NLP pipelines that ran in production for hundreds of companies. I built financial anomaly detection agents, support triage chatbots, and issue severity classifiers — systems where getting things right actually mattered.

I am also a Course Instructor for ESE 224 (Data Structures and Algorithms in C++) at Stony Brook, where I mentor 50+ students per semester through code reviews, assignments, and Socratic office hours designed to build genuine problem-solving ability.

Research

Research Areas

My work bridges NLP, behavioral science, and AI systems to understand and improve how engineering teams collaborate.

🎙️

Speech Analytics & Team Dynamics

Building SpeakMap, an end-to-end platform using Whisper large-v3, pyannote 3.1, and librosa to extract 8 behavioral metrics per speaker from real engineering team sessions. Applied to 24 undergraduate teams across 4 quarterly sessions.

Whisper pyannote librosa diarization
📊

Team Modeling & Prediction

Using Random Forest, Logistic Regression, OLS regression, and MLPRegressor to predict team-level dynamics outcomes including coordination, role emergence, and balanced participation from individual behavioral signals over time.

Random Forest MLPRegressor LOOCV FDR
🤖

LLM-Driven Collaboration (HILP)

Investigating Human-in-the-Loop Programming frameworks where LLMs act as intelligent facilitators during collaborative software development, generating real-time interventions that support team problem-solving and coordination.

LLMs HILP GPT API intervention
🔗

Neurosymbolic AI

Combining MLP neural feature learning with Decision Tree rule extraction to generate interpretable, actionable policies for LLM-driven collaboration interventions — making team dynamics models explainable and actionable.

neurosymbolic decision trees interpretability
